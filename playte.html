PLAYTE

A non-intrusive dieting iOS app that helps busy people think about their food without draining their time and energy. I was the sole designer and did all the research, designing and testing using Figma, Sketch, whimsical.io, usability hub, Maze and Draw.io

PROBLEM

Busy people often abandon diets because current dieting apps take too long and don’t understand users typical serving size.

SOLUTION

Combine machine learning with photogrammetry (3D objects from 2D photos) to create an app that recognizes the type and quantity of food users have on their plate.

USER RESEARCH

My assumptions:

  • Many users don’t understand the nutritional value of the food on their plate.

  • Accurately showing users the calories they eat will help them diet.

  • Most users will abandon diet apps that take more than minimal effort.

I created a survey to assess common dieting issues. Here’s a summary of what I found:

82%

Reported some sort of difficulty with understanding how many calories the food on their plate added up to.

46%

Said knowing what’s on their plate would be “very helpful” or “make their diet”.

51%

Would invest a max of 20 seconds in assessing their food.

Survey Results

I followed up the survey with user interviews to gain some context and give the quantitative data a qualitative edge. I found that:

  • Most people don’t know the nutritional value of the food on their plate.

  • A big turn off with diet apps is the degree of user input required to log food.

  • How the app works is far less important than getting people to think about their food. It’s all about creating a lifestyle change.

"How a diet app works doesn’t matter. The point is it gets people to think about their food”

-Raymond Sultan, RPA-C, Premium Health

Learnings:
My hypothesis was wrong. It’s not about quantifying every calorie, but helping users think about their food. The challenge is creating an app so fast and painless, users continue using it and by extension, continue thinking about their food.

Solution
Create a diet app that focuses on helping users consistently log their food. That requires making the logging process so fast and easy that it requires minimal input from users and what’s faster than just snapping a photo? Machine learning will recognize the food in the photo and photogrammetry (the process of creating 3D objects from 2D images) will allow the app to understand basic serving size.

I considered gamifying the app by rewarding users for every log but didn’t out of fear that rewarding logging would cause users to eat more so they could collect the reward. Ethical design includes understanding the potential for misuse or unintended consequences so I wanted to think through rewarding logs better before including it in the app.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

I studied Calorie Moma because it’s the most famous for its machine learning, and Lose It! together with MyFitnessPal because they’re the most popular diet apps in the iOS store.

View SWOT

Calorie Moma has strong image recognition options that recognizes a huge variety of foods paired with a slick UI and is cheaper than some competitors.

Most features require relatively expensive subscription and significant amounts of user interaction, photo recognition still gets fooled.

MyFitness Pal has a huge online community that keeps users motivated, exercised focused and synchronizes with over 50 apps.

No image recognition, homepage bloated with adds, requires the most user interaction, competitors have cleaner UI.

Lose It! Is the top iOS app in “health and fitness”, has effective image recognition, isn’t locked to a particular diet type and a simple UI.

Most expensive, intrusive ads, not designed for maintaining or gaining weight.

USER STORIES & FLOWS

I wrote the user stories and to best figure out the user flows, I downloaded competitors apps and screenshot the onboarding process. User testing revealed my recent project Photogration had a confusing onboarding so I wanted to make this experience seamless and transparent.

Competitors Onboarding Process

Many of the diet apps required users create a custom diet plan by answering a questionnaire. This is noteworthy because most apps avoid unnecessary text input which frustrates users and lengthens the time to the dashboard, increasing bounce rate. This tells me competitors believed the Aha! moment (when the value of an app becomes clear to the user) required a customized diet plan.

While the true value of Playte is helping users think about their food, the initial perceived value is quantifying users intake and contrasting it with their suggested intake. Therefore, Playte’s perceived value required a custom diet during onboarding too.

INITIAL TESTING

I focused on Playte’s most important features and areas I was concerned about for my in-person user testing. I was curious whether my “Next” button made text input smoother, if the lack of a menu would make the app confusing and whether the app was easy to use when fully populated.

Users were asked to:

  • Open the app and make their way to the homepage

  • Photograph a food an explain how they would correct a mis-identified food

  • Indicate how they would change account information

  • Explain what the graphs on the homepage meant

  • Navigate through their logs on a fully populated homepage

Prototype

I found three main issues:

INACCURATE CALORIE TRACKER

Before

This graph adds very little value because:

  • Graph isn’t accurate. Users aren’t eating 2,300 calories at midnight, another 1,800 at 2 AM etc.

  • Numbers make the graph feel crowded

  • There’s no legend explaining green and red bullets represent logs.

After

Users found this graph more useful because:

  • Graph starts at zero

  • Legend explains that bullets represent logs

  • Hours not as crowded

Changing the graph was an easy fix but users really struggled with text input. Inspired by the Acorns app which used “Next” to help users navigate between fields, I was aiming for something similar but it backfired on me.

NAVIGATING THROUGH SCREENS AND INPUT FIELDS

Before

Users struggled with text input because:

  • Users weren’t sure which box they were typing in

  • Users thought “Next” meant they finished that input field when they weren’t and got stuck when “Next” didn’t advance them.

  • Some preferred to tap on the relevant input field instead of clicking “Next”

  • Focusing users attention by greying out non-active boxes made it seem like already entered information was lost.

After

Users found text input much easier because:

  • Users can navigate by tapping on a field or using the “Next” button

  • Button only appears in the active state

  • Blue stroke indicates which field is active without modifying previous input field’s color.

It was interesting to see how even users who insisted they’d rather tap on an input field instead of using “Next” switched off between the two in the new version.

TEDIOUS ONBOARDING PROCESS

Before

Users were bouncing during the custom diet onboarding process because:

  • The 8 page custom diet creation is too long

  • No option to create a basic diet guide

After

Creating a basic diet which users can refine improved bounce rate because:

  • Only 3 pages long

  • Allows users to refine the diet by answering more questions

  • Remaining questions are only 6 pages long instead of original 8.

  • Users more likely to refine diet because already invested time in custom diet creation.



BRANDING & HI-FI FRAMES

Though I started with gradients, I found switching to a simpler, fresh and minimalist approach helped accessability. I chose postive, bright colors with a strong purple highlight to lend a playful vibe.

Similarly, I chose Poppins as the sole font because of its playful yet legible nature. Its near perfect circles resemble a plate and its geometric shape compliments the modern feel.

I designed two logos going in different directions but each had a strength weakness.

Omelet is unique and fun. Scales well at different sizes and color schemes.

Not as obviously linked to “Playte”, links brand to eggs which some users may dislike or be allergic to. Grey is a little drab.

P clearly connects to name “Playte”, has a playful bend

Resembles other “P” logo’s and doesn’t scale well across color schemes

I decided on the omelet because it's more visually compelling and won’t be easily confused for other brands. Given more time I would iterate further or create a third option.

PROJECT REFLECTIONS

It’s hard to summarize everything I learnt into a few bullet points, just as the contents of this case study barely scratch the surface of everything that went into this project. A master comparison of how the app evolved can be found here .

Some general takeaways:

  1. Design and prototyping tools can sometimes introduce friction where a functional app wouldn’t have any.

  2. Think to the extremes. What does an app look like after 6 months of use? How far back will the app record? Will the navigational elements function at that level?

  3. Some color combinations will have accessibility problems no matter how you wrangle them and a new approach may be necessary.

  4. Getting feedback from senior designers can be a force multiplier that takes my designs to new heights.

  5. Don’t be afraid to defend your designs if you think you’re correct.

  6. Sometimes I’m wrong, and I have the A/B tests to prove it.
